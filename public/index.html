What Makes a Good Password?

      
A strong password is long, complex, and unpredictable. This is measured in "bits of entropy".

      
    
        
  • Length: This is the most important factor. Aim for 16 characters or more. A 12-character password is millions of times stronger than an 8-character one.
    • 
        
  • Complexity: Use a mix of uppercase letters (A-Z), lowercase letters (a-z), numbers (0-9), and symbols (!, @, #, $).
    • 
        
  • Unpredictability: Avoid common words, names, dates, or sequences like "12345" or "qwerty". The best passwords are completely random.
    • 
      

    


    

      
Advanced Password Concepts

      
Understanding *how* passwords are attacked helps you build better defenses.

      
      

        

          
Hashing vs. Encryption

          
Encryption is a two-way process. Data is scrambled and can be unscrambled with a secret key. This is used for sending secret messages. Hashing is a one-way process. It turns data into a unique "fingerprint" that cannot be reversed. This is how services should store your password. They hash your login attempt and see if the hash matches the one they have stored.

        

        
        

          
Salt & Pepper

          
A **"salt"** is a random, unique value added to your password *before* it's hashed. This is stored with your user info. It stops attackers from using pre-computed "rainbow tables" to crack common passwords. A **"pepper"** is a secret, site-wide key added before hashing. It adds another layer of security, but the salt is more critical for individual account protection.

        

        
        

          
Brute Force vs. Dictionary Attack

          
A **Brute Force** attack tries every possible combination of characters (e.g., `a`, `aa`, `ab`, `ac`). This is slow but will *eventually* find any password. A **Dictionary Attack** uses a list of common words, phrases, and known leaked passwords (like `password123` or `Fluffy`). This is much faster and more effective against weak passwords.

        

        
        

          
Credential Stuffing

          
This is the most common attack today. Attackers take lists of leaked usernames and passwords from one data breach (e.g., a 2014 forum hack) and "stuff" them into other login forms (like your email or bank). This works because *people re-use passwords*. This is why a password manager and unique passwords for every site are essential.
